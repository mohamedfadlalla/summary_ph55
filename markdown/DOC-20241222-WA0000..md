This document provides information on pancreatic hormones, their physiological roles, and the classification and diagnosis of Diabetes Mellitus.

# Pancreatic Hormones and Anti-diabetic Drugs

## The Endocrine Pancreas
The endocrine pancreas in the adult human consists of approximately one million **islets of Langerhans** interspersed throughout the pancreatic gland. The islets contain four main cell types, all of which secrete peptide hormones:

1.  **B (β-cell):**
    *   **Insulin.**
    *   **Islet amyloid polypeptide (IAPP, or amylin):** Cosecreted with insulin in a ratio of approximately 100:1. Amylin plays a role in glycemic regulation by preventing post-prandial rise in blood glucose levels by:
        *   Slowing gastric emptying and promoting satiety.
        *   Inhibiting glucagon secretion.
2.  **A (α) cells:**
    *   **Glucagon:** A hyperglycemic factor that mobilizes glycogen stores.
3.  **D (δ) cells:**
    *   **Somatostatin:** A universal inhibitor of secretory cells. It inhibits the release of glucagon, insulin, growth hormone (GH), and gastrin.
4.  **F (or PP) cells:**
    *   **Pancreatic polypeptide (PP):** A small protein that facilitates digestive processes.

### Table: Pancreatic Islet Cells and Their Secretory Products
| Cell Types | Approximate Percent of Islet Mass | Secretory Products |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Alpha (A) cell | 20% | Glucagon, proglucagon |
| Beta (B) cell | 75% | Insulin, C-peptide, proinsulin, amylin |
| Delta (D) cell | 3–5% | Somatostatin |
| G cell | 1% | Gastrin |
| F cell (PP cell) | 1% | Pancreatic polypeptide (PP) |

## Control of Blood Glucose
Physiological control of blood glucose is required to maintain an adequate fuel supply to the brain.

*   **Insulin:** The most important regulatory hormone. Increased blood glucose (hyperglycemia) stimulates insulin secretion and vice versa.
*   **Counter-regulatory Hormones:** Hypoglycemia, in addition to reducing insulin secretion, elicits the secretion of hormones that increase blood glucose:
    *   Glucagon
    *   Adrenaline (Epinephrine)
    *   Glucocorticoids
    *   Growth hormone

## Diabetes Mellitus (DM)
Diabetes Mellitus is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by hyperglycemia caused by relative or absolute insulin deficiency, with or without concurrent impairment of insulin action (resistance).

### Global Statistics (IDF Diabetes Atlas 2019)
*   Approximately 463 million adults (20-79 years) were living with diabetes; projected to rise to 700 million by 2045.
*   79% of adults with diabetes live in low- and middle-income countries.
*   1 in 5 people above 65 years old have diabetes.
*   1 in 2 people (232 million) with diabetes are undiagnosed.
*   Diabetes caused 4.2 million deaths in 2019.

### Symptoms and Complications
*   **Symptoms:** Glycosuria, Polyuria, Polydipsia.
*   **Acute Complications:**
    *   Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA).
    *   Hyperglycemic hyperosmolar nonketotic syndrome.
*   **Chronic Vascular Complications:**
    *   **Microvascular:** Retinopathy, Nephropathy, Neuropathy.
    *   **Macrovascular:** Cardiovascular, cerebrovascular, and peripheral vascular diseases.

## Classification of Diabetes

### 1. Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus
*   Formerly known as Insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus (IDDM) or juvenile-onset diabetes.
*   Severe or absolute deficiency of insulin resulting from **pancreatic β cell destruction**.
*   Accounts for 5%–10% of DM cases.
*   **Subdivisions:**
    *   **Type 1A:** Immune-mediated; most common; presents in childhood but can occur at any stage (e.g., LADA - Latent Autoimmune Diabetes of the Adult).
    *   **Type 1B:** Idiopathic diabetes.

### 2. Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus
*   Formerly known as Non-insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus (NIDDM) or maturity-onset diabetes.
*   Accounts for 90%–95% of DM cases.
*   Characterized by **insulin resistance** combined with a relative deficiency in insulin secretion.
*   Strongly associated with obesity and physical inactivity.
*   **Treatment:** Initially dietary/lifestyle changes, then oral hypoglycemic drugs; about one-third of patients eventually require insulin.

### 3. Maturity-Onset Diabetes of the Young (MODY)
*   A genetic disorder leading to impaired insulin secretion with little to no impairment in insulin action.
*   Accounts for 1%–2% of all diabetes; onset is usually before age 25.

### 4. Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM)
*   Glucose intolerance occurring during pregnancy, most commonly in the third trimester.
*   **Risk Factors:** Obesity, older age at pregnancy, family history of type 2 diabetes.
*   **Risks:** Gestational hypertension, preeclampsia, macrosomia in offspring, and a 7-fold increased risk for the mother to develop diabetes later in life.
*   **Management:** Lifestyle changes are essential. Insulin is the preferred medication as it does not cross the placenta.

### 5. Other Specific Types
*   Caused by pancreatectomy, diseases of the exocrine pancreas (pancreatitis, cystic fibrosis), or drug/chemical-induced (glucocorticoids, atypical antipsychotics).

## Methods and Criteria for Diagnosis of DM

| Test | Diabetes Criteria | Prediabetes Criteria |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Random Blood Sugar** | ≥ 11.1 mmol/L (200 mg/dL) + symptoms | — |
| **Fasting Plasma Glucose (FPG)** | ≥ 7.0 mmol/L (126 mg/dL) | 5.6 to 6.9 mmol/L (100–125 mg/dL) |
| **Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT)** | ≥ 11.1 mmol/L (200 mg/dL) 2 hrs post-75g glucose | 7.8 to 11.0 mmol/L (140–199 mg/dL) |
| **Hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c)** | ≥ 6.5% (48 mmol/mol) | 5.7% to 6.4% (39–47 mmol/mol) |

*   **HbA1c Advantages:** Does not require fasting; less day-to-day variability.
*   **HbA1c Disadvantages:** May be inaccurate in patients with hemolytic anemia, chronic malaria, sickle cell anemia, or recent blood transfusions.

## Insulin: Synthesis and Action

### Chemistry
Insulin is a small protein (molecular weight ~5808) containing **51 amino acids** arranged in two peptide chains:
*   **A chain:** 21 amino acids.
*   **B chain:** 30 amino acids.
*   The chains are linked by disulfide bridges.

### Synthesis
1.  Synthesized as **preproinsulin** in the rough endoplasmic reticulum.
2.  Processed in the Golgi apparatus to **proinsulin**.
3.  Cleaved into **insulin** and a residual **C-peptide**.
4.  Stored in granules in B cells and cosecreted in equimolar amounts.
5.  Stored as crystals consisting of two zinc atoms and six insulin molecules (hexamer).

### Mechanism of Secretion
*   Hyperglycemia leads to increased intracellular ATP in β-cells.
*   ATP blocks **ATP-sensitive K+ channels**, causing depolarization.
*   Depolarization opens **voltage-gated Ca2+ channels**.
*   Inward Ca2+ flux triggers the exocytosis of insulin granules.

### Endocrine and Anabolic Effects
Insulin is an anabolic hormone promoting fuel conservation:
*   **Carbohydrate Metabolism:** Stimulates glucose uptake (via GLUT-4 in muscle and fat) and glycogenesis; inhibits glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis.
*   **Fat Metabolism:** Increases lipogenesis and inhibits lipolysis.
*   **Protein Metabolism:** Stimulates amino acid uptake and protein synthesis; inhibits protein breakdown.

### Mechanism of Action
Insulin binds to a specific **tyrosine kinase-linked receptor** consisting of 2α and 2β subunits. Binding triggers autophosphorylation of the β subunits, recruiting **Insulin Receptor Substrate (IRS)** proteins. This initiates signaling cascades (like the Ras-MAPK pathway) that regulate glucose transporters, metabolic enzymes, and gene expression.
